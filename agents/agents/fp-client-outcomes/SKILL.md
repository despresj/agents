# First Principles — Business, Sales & Client Outcomes

Apply the `first-principles` core skill. Use this pack for client-outcome traps
and precondition templates; use the core once for checkpoints and reporting.

## The Governing Facts

1. **Clients buy a changed number, not a deliverable.** A model, dashboard, or
   report has value only through a decision someone makes differently. If no
   decision changes, perfect execution still produces zero value — and the
   client eventually notices.
2. **The request is the client's hypothesis, not their problem.** "Build us a
   segmentation model" is a solution someone already chose, encoding an
   unvalidated diagnosis. The frame audit (O5) here is literal: what happened
   in their business that made someone ask, and what number are they trying to
   move?
3. **Value is denominated in the client's units.** Revenue, cost, margin, risk,
   time — theirs, with their baseline numbers. Accuracy, features, and hours
   are vendor units; a case built in vendor units collapses at the client's
   first internal review.
4. **The requester is rarely the whole client.** The champion who asked, the
   economic buyer who pays, and the users whose behavior must change each have
   a different definition of success. An engagement that satisfies one and
   ignores the others fails in review, renewal, or adoption respectively.

## Trap Table

| Symptom | Tempting move | First-principles move | Discriminating test |
|---------|--------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Client asks for solution X | Scope and build X | Trace X back to the decision and number it is believed to move | "If X works perfectly, which metric moves, by roughly how much, and who acts differently?" No answer → discovery isn't done |
| Vague outcome language ("better marketing") | Restate it in the proposal | Vague goals cannot fail — and cannot be renewed on | Convert to a named metric with a baseline and a date before pricing anything |
| Proposal drafted as activities | List phases, meetings, deliverables | Anchor on outcomes, with activities as the mechanism | Each phase names the client-unit result it exists to produce; a phase without one is padding |
| Success undefined at kickoff | Start work, define later | Unbaselined work is unprovable work — you can't show a delta without a before | Baseline measured and success criteria signed before the first sprint |
| Champion enthusiasm | Read it as deal/renewal signal | Map the buying system: economic buyer, blockers, process, do-nothing option | Has the economic buyer stated *their* success criterion? Enthusiasm ≠ authority |
| ROI case built to impress | Vendor-authored assumptions, best case | Co-build with the client's own numbers, conservatively — they must defend it internally without you | Would their CFO accept every input? Which assumption, if halved, kills the case? |
| Pilot succeeded → assume rollout | Extrapolate pilot results | Pilots select friendly users, clean data, and hand-holding — name what changes at scale | List pilot-vs-production differences; which were load-bearing for the result? |
| Scope creep from a happy client | Accept to preserve the relationship | Each addition maps to the agreed outcome or trades against something | "Does this move the number we signed up for? If not, what do we drop or re-price?" |
| Objection raised | Counter it with rebuttals | An objection is data about a risk they see and you don't | Investigate what they're protecting; steelman it (O3) — they may be right |
| Deal/renewal all-positive but stalled | Push harder, discount | The strongest rival to "yes" is "do nothing" — status quo has zero switching cost | What does inaction cost them, in their numbers, by when? If nothing → there is no deal |
| Client "knows their problem" | Take the diagnosis as given | Symptoms travel up orgs faster than causes | Ask for the triggering incident: what happened, to whom, costing what? |
| Analysis readout impressed everyone, nothing changed | Count it as a win; send the next analysis | An insight without an owner, decision, and deadline is entertainment | Every finding leaves the room attached to: who decides what, by when, measured how? |
| Price anchored on effort | Cost-plus / day-rate math | Price against the value at stake and the do-nothing cost | Is the fee small relative to the conservatively-estimated outcome? If unknowable, discovery isn't done |

## Precondition Template (O1)

**"This proposal/engagement will produce client value":** the target metric is
named in client units with a measured baseline and review date; the decisions
that will change, and who makes them, are identified; the causal chain
deliverable → changed behavior → moved number is explicit, with an owner for
every link the vendor doesn't control; the economic buyer's success criterion
is known from them, not inferred; the do-nothing counterfactual is stated and
costed; measurement is agreed before work begins, not reconstructed after.

## Domain Escalation Triggers

- No one can name the number that moves → stop scoping; run discovery on the
  triggering incident, not the requested solution.
- Success criteria drift mid-engagement → re-run the frame audit with the
  economic buyer before absorbing the change.
- The value case only works with the vendor's optimistic inputs → the case is
  yours, not theirs; it will not survive their internal review.
- Adoption depends on client behavior change nobody owns → the engagement's
  weakest link is outside your control; surface it in writing now, not at QBR.
